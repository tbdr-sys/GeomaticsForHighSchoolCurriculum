Glossary of Terms

abiotic

referring to non-living things such as land, climate, water

abyssal plain

flat areas on the deep ocean floor

acid rain

rain water that is contaminated by sulfur dioxide and nitric oxide in the atmosphere. It is corrosive in nature and will cause vegetation to die and lakes to become acidic.

active layer

upper layer of permafrost that thaws only during the brief summer

air mass

large body of air that has the same moisture and temperature throughout and that affects weather conditions

air pressure

weight of the air mass

anticlinal trap

dome-shaped structure of rock layers created by tectonic plate movement. Oil and gas are often trapped in these folds.

archipelago

a group of many islands

Arctic

the northern polar regions

base map

a map showing only the basic outline of features

bedrock

a mass of solid rock underlying surficial material

biome

an area with characteristic geographic and climatic conditions that support certain animal and plant populations

bioregion

a region defined by its natural and human characteristics that supports an ecological community

biotic

referring to living organisms such as plants and animals

bog

soft, wet, spongy ground found next to shallow lakes and ponds and filled with decaying vegetation, often covered by moss

boreal forest

a forest consisting mainly of coniferous trees that stretches across Canada from east to west

Canadian Shield

a large geological region of Precambrian rock in central Canada. It is the oldest rock formation on Earth.

cash crop

crops, such as corn and grains, specifically grown by farmers to be sold

clear-cutting

the logging of a forest area by cutting all trees at the same time

climate

weather conditions of a place averaged over a long period of time

coastal marsh

a wetland along an ocean coast where salt and fresh water mix

condensation

a process whereby water vapour is cooled and changed to liquid. Clouds and fog are condensed water vapour.

coniferous tree

a softwood tree that has needlelike leaves and bears cones. Evergreens such as pine, fir and spruce are examples.

continental shelves

gently sloping seabed that extends from the continent beneath the ocean to a maximum depth of about 200m

continental slope

the steep slope that extends from the continental shelf to the ocean depths of the abyssal plain

cordillera

a group of usually parallel mountain ranges

correlation

a relationship between two things

cross-section

a diagram that shows an object or landform from the perspective of being cut in half

Crown lands

land owned by the government

database

information that is set up in a table format that can be used by computers. The format allows information to be sorted, searched and rearranged.

deciduous tree

broad leaved trees that shed their leaves in the fall. Birch, maple and oak are examples.

deforestation

the destruction of a forest by removal of trees and plants

degraded land

productive land that is rendered unproductive by human activity such as urban use

discharge rate

the amount of water that flows through a system in a given time

drainage

the flow of water away from an area

drainage basin

an area surrounded by higher land that catches rain water and is drained away by a system of rivers and tributaries

drainage divide

the high point of land that divides drainage basins

drainage system

the network of streams and rivers that collect and direct the runoff from the land to a river system

drumlin

a geological feature in the shape of a low oval mound formed when a glacier pushed boulders ahead of it

drought

an extended period of time in which there is no precipitation

ecodistricts

an area of land having specific ecological factors. It is the next subdivision of an ecoregion. There are 1021 ecodistricts in Canada.

ecological footprint

the measure of total human impact on an ecosystem

ecoregion

one of 217 subdivisions of the ecozones of Canada

ecosystem

a unit of nature comprised of organisms (including people), the physical environment (land, water, climate) and the relationships that exist between them.

ecozone

a large order ecosystem in a landscape where a fairly definitive and enduring mix of abiotic and biotic elements exist. There are 15 terrestrial ecozones and 5 marine ecozones in Canada.

end moraine

a ridge-like accumulation that is being or was produced at the outer margin of an actively flowing glacier at any given time.

era

a large division of time related historically

erosion

the process by which ice, wind or water wears away land surfaces and transports the debris to other locations

Erratic

Rock picked up by the ice, carried along, and deposited many kilometers away; easily identifiable because its composition is different from the bed rock of the region in which it was deposited.

eskers

low winding ridge of gravel formed by the deposition of material by streams running beneath a glacier

exports

commodities produced in one country for sale in another country

faulting

movement of the earth’s surface along cracks in the earth’s crust

fault line

a crack in the earth’s crust

fauna

the animal life

federal government

a system of government in which several provincial and territorial governments form a unity but remain independent in internal affairs. It is responsible for the creation and enforcement of national laws and policies for Canada

fiord

long, narrow inlet of the sea with steep sides created by glaciers

flood

an overflow of water beyond its normal confines such as lakes and rivers

flora

the plant life

fodder crop

plants grown for feeding animals such as hay, clover, alfalfa

fossil fuel

any mineral that can be burned to produce energy such as coal, oil, natural gas

frost

water vapour that has condensed into feathery ice crystals on a solid surface under freezing temperature

geographic information system (GIS)

integrated software package for the input, management, analysis and display of spatial information

geological time

history of the earth’s formation which can be divided into major era: Cenozoic era (most recent 66 million years)

Mesozoic era (245 million to 66 million years ago)

Paleozoic era (570 million to 245 million years ago)

Precambrian era (4600 million to 570 million years ago)

glacier

a large mass of ice which flows slowly as a sheet or river

global warming

rising world average annual temperatures due to the increasing levels of greenhouse gases (carbon dioxide, methane, water vapour, etc) in the atmosphere

Geomatics

is the science and technology of gathering, analyzing, interpreting, distributing and using geographic information

Greenhouse gas

gases related to pollution that cause a blanket effect around the earth’s atmosphere

Groundwater

water that is found below the earth’s surface

growing season

the period during which crops can grow. In Canada it is the number of day on which the temperature rises above 5.6 degree C

Herbivores

animals that feed on plants

highlands

areas of high elevation containing mountains and plateau

hurricanes

tropical cyclones or storms with exceptionally strong winds

hythergraph

a grid that shows temperature and precipitation. It is useful for comparing the climate of two or more places.

Ice Age

a period of time when freezing temperatures created ice sheets over continents. Glaciers covered most of Canada during the last period that was 6000 to 10,000 years ago.

ice sheet

very large thick mass of glacier ice flowing outward in all directions from an area of heavy snow accumulation

igneous rock

rock formed from cooling lava or magma (melted rock)

import

products and services that are brought into a country from another country

indigenous

people, flora and fauna belonging naturally to a region

International Date Line

an imaginary line that generally follows the 180 degree line of longitude where one day ends and the next begins. East of the line is one day earlier than it is to the west. This line run through the Pacific Ocean west of Australia and diverts around some island groups. It was adopted internationally in 1884.

intrusion

lava that has forced its way through the cracks in the earth’s crust

infrastructure

the networks of public services that are required to sustain economic and societal activities (transportation, communication, education)

inorganic

containing no oxygen in their chemical composition

jet stream

west to east movement of air in the mid-latitude at altitudes of between 8 and 15 kilometers at speeds of up to 400 km/h.

lake plain

fertile bottom of an ancient glacier lake used for agriculture

land capability

ability of land to sustain a certain purpose, e.g. agriculture needs good soil, drainage, certain climatic conditions

land claims

the formal demands by Aboriginal People for control and ownership of lands on which they live or have traditionally lived

landforms

natural features such as mountains, valleys or plains on the earth’s surface

land use

how ground and soil are used for urban or rural activities. (e.g., housing, forestry, agriculture, parks)

latitude

imaginary lines circling the globe measuring the distance in degrees north or south of the equator. The equator is at 0 degrees, the North Pole 900 north latitude, the South Pole 900 south latitude.

leaching

the percolating of water through material such as soil and vegetation to removal salts and minerals

leeward

the side of a mountain or mountain range sheltered from the prevailing winds

lichen

small, slow growing and very hardy plant organisms that are usually found on bare rocks or tree trunks

loam

rich fertile soil composed of decaying plant matter, clay, silt and sand

longitude

imaginary lines running north and south that are used to measure in degrees the distance east or west of the Prime Meridian at 00 longitude. This line coincides with the International Date Line on the Atlantic side of the globe.

lowlands

areas of low elevation containing flat lands and hills.

magma

molten rock beneath the earth’s crust

map projection

method of using mathematical calculations to transfer the features of the spheroid shaped globe to a flat map surface.

map scale

the ratio of measurements used to reduce the real distances on the Earth’s surface to one that can be used on a map

maritime

living or found near the sea, area of land attached to the sea

maritime climate

weather conditions influenced by the closeness of an ocean or other large body of water which tends to moderate temperature extremes and have high moisture content to produce rain and fog

marsh

soft, wet land found in low-lying areas with poor drainage caused by underlying soils such as clay

metamorphic rock

type of rock formed when sedimentary and igneous rocks are subjected to great heat and pressure

methane

simplest hydrocarbon gas that is colourless, odourless and flammable

migration

movement of large numbers of species from one location to another (e.g., birds, mammals, people)

mixed forest

a vegetation transition zone in which both deciduous and coniferous trees grow

moderating effect

the effect of large bodies of water to absorb heat and moderate the temperature of surrounding areas from extreme hot or cold

montane

referring to mountainous regions

moraine

glacier deposits of rock and debris in the form of hills

mountain

a large natural elevation of the earth’s surface rising abruptly from the surrounding level

muskeg

a level swamp or bog in Canada

natural vegetation

plants that are native to an area and were not brought in by human intervention

National Parks Act

a federal government law to set aside areas of land for the Canadian people’s benefit , enjoyment and education. Passed in 1930

non-renewable resources

natural resources that cannot be replaced and are limited in quantity such as fossil fuels (oil, natural gas and coal)

oil sands

naturally occurring stratum of porous rock which contains heavy crude oil and water

old-growth forest

a mature forest that is in its natural state and has never been logged

ozone layer

a region of the stratosphere about 10 km above the Earth that contains a high concentration of ozone gas, O3 ,formed by the effects of the sun’s ultraviolet radiation on oxygen.

permafrost

ground that is permanently frozen. In some areas the top layer will thaw temporarily in summer

plankton

microscopic plants and animals found floating in the sea or fresh water

plate tectonics

theory that the earth’s crust consists of plates that move causing earthquakes, volcanoes, mountains

plain

a level tract of treeless land

plateau

an area of fairly level high ground

polar

pertaining to the climatic zone north of the Arctic Circle (66.50N) or south of the Arctic Circle (66.50 S).

pollution

contamination or destruction of the earth’s environment

population density

a mathematical value calculated to determine the average number of people living within a unit area, usually people per square kilometer

Pingo

A large frost mound that has been raised in part by hydrostatic pressure within and below the permafrost of Arctic regions.

prairie

large area of usually treeless grassland found mainly in North America

prevailing winds

the direction from which the wind usually blows. In Canada, the winds are usually Westerlies, that is, blowing from west to east.

Prime Meridian

line of longitude running through Greenwich, England ( 00 )from which all other longitudes are measured, 1800 east and 1800 west

productive land

land which is needed to support human activities

rain shadow

area on the leeward side of a mountain that receives little precipitation

relief

the difference in elevation between features on the Earth’s surface

remote sensing

images taken of the earth’s surface from satellites and aircraft circling the globe

renewable resource

a resource that can be regenerated or replenished e.g. trees, solar or wind energy

reserve

an area of land set aside for a specific purpose

reservoir

artificial lake built by construction of a dam to back up a river and to collect water from precipitation for hydro-electric generation, recreation, flood control, irrigation and consumption

rift valley

steep sided valley that is formed when the portion of land between two nearly parallel faults subsides. An example is the St. Lawrence River valley

river flood plain

flat land bordering a river that is subject to flooding

runoff

water that flows over the earth’s surface and is not absorbed into the ground

rural

areas outside of towns or cities used for agriculture or pasture

sediment

material deposited by the movement of wind, water or ice

sedimentary rock

rock formed by the compression of layers of sediments over very long periods of time

shield

large area of Precambrian rock that forms the core of the continent

slope

area of rising or falling ground

soil erosion

soil that is removed by natural causes such as wind and water

soil profile

the different horizons (layers) in the soil and the rock layer (bedrock) below the soil. Each horizon has different physical, biological and chemical properties. visible in a cross-section of the earth

species

groups of living organisms that have some common characteristics and capable of interbreeding

sustainability

goal of development that meets the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs

swamp

low-lying wetland that is overgrown with vegetation, mainly caused by poor drainage

taiga

coniferous forest land

tar sands

mixture of sand, heavy crude oil and water

tectonic forces

forces exerted by the coming together of the tectonic plates causing folding, breaking and warping of the Earth’s crust

tectonic plates

large continental sized pieces of the earth’s crust that float on the molten mantle

temperate

the mid-latitudes between the Arctic Circle and the Tropic of Cancer that have distinct spring and fall seasons

thematic map

a map showing only one theme or topic such as forestry

topographic map

a detailed large-scale map showing the relative positions, elevations and surface features of places or regions

transition zone

an area which contains a mixture of natural vegetation, wildlife and soils found in the two adjacent regions

tree line

marks the boundary between the boreal forest and the tundra zones. North of this line climatic conditions severely restrict the height of trees

tundra

treeless plains of northern North America surrounding the Arctic Circle. Weather conditions of extreme cold and wind with short cool summers make tree growth impossible.

urbanization

process of changing from rural to city or town

U-shaped valley

valley formed by alpine glaciers moving down and scraping the sides of a valley to produce a U shape

water table

the level to which water saturates the soil zone

watershed

an area drained by a lake or river and its tributaries

weather

combination of temperature, precipitation, wind and cloud cover that describes the conditions of the atmosphere at any given time

wetlands

areas where the water table level is at or above the surrounding land area most of the year (e.g., swamp, bog, marsh, muskeg)

wilderness

area of uninhabited and uncultivated land where humans have not taken over

windward

the side that is exposed to the wind

wind erosion

wearing away of soil or destruction of landforms by the force of the wind

World Heritage Site

selected places on the Earth that are protected by international law for the benefit of humans for all time

zone

an area having particular properties, features, purpose or use
